Bangalore by Bike

Developing a cycle culture for the future

Bangalore's rapid growth has led to traffic congestion, air pollution, and environmental degradation. Developing sustainable transportation options, such as cycling and public transport, can reduce the city's carbon footprint and improve the quality of life.
This UX case study focuses on a self‐service, bike‐sharing scheme inspired by the Velib cycling network in Paris, to improve the deteriorating quality of life.

The Approach

Focusing on features Goals

Although my Initial idea was to develop better functionality than the current players, with more clarity it was obvious engaging in a feature parity war was not strategic. Whilst the current players are focusing on taking the pain out of the scheme, my vision was to help users get more pleasure out of it. I wanted this to become an enabler for a better Bangalore.

Stephen Anderson's UX Hierarchy of Needs heavily influenced the product strategy.

The Discovery

What does 'Ride' mean to you?

Research revealed that the concept of 'ride' represented something different to users. User’s motivations for riding and participating in the scheme differed, hinting at different requirements. After designating persona types and aligning this with the phasing strategy I could prioritise who to focus on supporting in the early stages. The phase 1 app focused on supporting the goals of Suhail and Ananya, the primary personas.

Persona hypothesis consisted of five different archetypes which was used to facilitate discovery about users needs, desires and varying contexts of use. Sufficient behavioural variables to segment user audience was identified. These variables could be categorised into activities such as frequency of use of the scheme, skills such as riding confidence and motivations such as reasons for cycling.

The Vision

A Better Bangalore–United by Bike

Minimise the Bad

Key priority for the early phases was to focus on primary user goals. Our first task–stripping the app to its core and focus on usability and convenience.

Amplify the Good

Beyond the obvious features, we saw opportunities to build a deep emotional connection with our users. Our vision was to create an honest, human app.

Unifying Bangalore

We envisioned empowering London's city of cycling culture, where cyclists could co‐create safer cycling routes and contribute to the community.

To communicate the personality of the app, a set of experience principles are required. These are used to sense‐check design decisions, articulate core values and describe key attributes the app experience that should uphold for the users. The principles are used constantly to drive the aesthetic, feel and overall tonal direction of the app.

Make important things fast

The main screen has been designed to allow users quick access to primary functions of the application.

Minimise learning

primarily designed to support people on-the-go, users can depend on their prior experience with Google Maps and existing iOS design conventions to help them learn to use the app

Designed for Confidence

The interface design strives to be confident. It does not contain UI‐bling or unnecessary elements. We opted for clear, readable typography —choosing colours with high contrast to increase legibility in outdoor, low‑light conditions. The design is uncluttered, clean, large and well spaced. All our design decisions help to exude a sense of confidence in the design.
